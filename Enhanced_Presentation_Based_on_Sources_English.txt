This question sounds simple, but it has generated sustained discussion for decades. One camp, which we might call the "gift" school, argues that Buddhist dana is essentially selfless, should not expect reciprocity, and follows the original intent of Buddhist doctrine. Another camp, the "exchange" school, argues that regardless of what people say, actual donation practices always expect returnsâ€”whether social prestige in this life or blessings in the next.

French anthropologist Nicolas SihlÃ©, in his 2015 Religion Compass article, precisely captures this issue. He writes: "We need to argue for the analytical importance of the distinction between 'gifts' strictly speaking and patterns of transfers that are more akin to an exchange or transaction." This points to the crux of the matter: when devotees support monks, and monks provide ritual services and bestow merit, is this a "selfless gift" or a "service transaction"?

To understand this debate, we must return to the anthropological classicâ€”Marcel Mauss's 1925 essay "The Gift." Mauss studied gift exchange worldwide, especially the potlatch rituals of North American indigenous peoples. He noted that potlatch consists of a festival where goods and services are exchanged, gifts are made and reciprocated with interest, with dominant ideas of rivalry and competition between tribes.

Mauss's core thesis: all gifts create reciprocal obligations. The obligation to give, the obligation to receive, the obligation to repayâ€”this is the famous "triple obligation."

Thank you to the first speaker. Now I'll discuss the scholarship on merit transfer, focusing on two classic articles: Gombrich's 1971 "Merit Transference in Sinhalese Buddhism" in History of Religions, and Holt's 1981 "Assisting the Dead by Venerating the Living" in Numen.

First, let's understand why "merit transfer" is academically significant. It involves a fundamental contradiction between Buddhist doctrine and practice.

According to orthodox Buddhist doctrine, each person is responsible for their own karma. The Buddha's solution to the problem of evil: one's suffering is due to former sin, well-being due to former goodness. Moreover, morality depends solely on intentionâ€”in Pali, "cetanÄ."

But here's the problem. Gombrich observed that in actual South Asian Buddhist practice, there exists what seems to contradict karma doctrine: "transference of merit"â€”transferring one's accumulated good karma to others, especially deceased relatives. This seems to violate the principle that each person is responsible for their own karma.

Gombrich uses an elegant metaphor: "one's good actions build up a kind of spiritual bank account from which one can make payments to others" (p.204). Merit becomes reifiedâ€”a "substance" that can be stored, calculated, and transferred.

How is this contradiction reconciled? Gombrich's core argument: merit transfer results from ancient ritual practices being "reinterpreted" under new ideology. As he notes at the paper's opening: "In the history of religions, it is a commonplace that practices survive the advent of new ideologies and are then reinterpreted" (p.203).

He uses an example familiar to Western readers: Christmas trees and Easter eggs were originally pagan ritual elements, yet they survived from paganism to Christianity, and in many cases from Christianity to secularism.

Now let's turn to Holt's 1981 paper "Assisting the Dead by Venerating the Living." The title itself is insightful: to help dead relatives, the method is to venerate living monksâ€”by supporting monks and transferring the resulting merit to the deceased.

Holt writes: "giving material amenities to the monastic community and merit to one's departed kin was evidence that the giver had cultivated a healthy mental disposition characterized by selflessness, compassion and charity" (p.1).

Crucially, Holt emphasizes the social dimension: "In exchange for receiving material amenities, the sheer presence of virtuous bhikkhus presented the laity with an opportunity to make merit" (p.2). This acknowledges the exchange nature of monastic-lay relationsâ€”though monks seem to only "receive" without "giving," monks actually "give" laity the opportunity to make merit through their presence and virtue.

This resonates with Walsh's argument.

Thank you to the second speaker. Now I'll introduce Katherine Bowie's 1998 American Anthropologist article "The Alchemy of Charity: Of Class and Buddhism in Northern Thailand." This paper examines merit practice from a class analysis perspective, providing an often-overlooked critical viewpoint.

Bowie challenges a traditional anthropological assumption at the paper's outset. She notes that scholars have typically made a "radical distinction between societies where gifts predominate and 'the class society, where private property and the commodity are the norm'" (p.469). In other words, gifts are assumed to matter only in "primitive" or "classless" societies.

But Bowie argues this assumption is wrong. She writes: "While no one would deny the centrality of gift giving in the political economy of egalitarian societies, scholars have ignored and even denied its significance in class-stratified societies" (p.469). Her paper corrects this bias.

Bowie's research is based on extensive fieldwork in Thailand's Chiang Mai Valley, interviewing over 500 elderly villagers across 400+ villages. The statistics are striking: in the 1970s, 34.9% of central plains rural households lived below the poverty line, 45.2% in the south, 63.6% in the north, and 74.7% in the northeast.

Her field findings are equally striking. Of 273 villages surveyed, only 9 villagesâ€”just 3.3%â€”had respondents claiming villagers were wholly "self-sufficient." In nearly half (48.7%) of villages, most households fell short of rice for at least two or three months each year.

In this context of class stratification, Bowie analyzes how merit practice operates. Her core argument: "cross-class, unidirectional forms of giving such as charity may be important in mediating the processes of hegemony and resistance in the sociopolitical constitution of complex societies" (p.469).

When a wealthy person donates lavishly to a temple, they gain two things: merit and social prestige. Merit is religious-level returnâ€”blessings in the next life; prestige is social-level returnâ€”status in this life. This donation actually legitimizes existing wealth disparity: the rich are rich because they accumulated merit in past lives; their donations now accumulate merit for future livesâ€”a self-reinforcing cycle.

Bowie cites Bourdieu: although Bourdieu portrays gift giving in precapitalist societies as "an elementary form of domination," he suggests state-based societies have developed more indirect, impersonal mechanisms (p.469). Bowie's addition: even in modern class society, religious charity remains an important mechanism for maintaining class hegemony.

This resonates with Walsh's analysis.
