Good morning/afternoon everyone. Today our group will present a fascinating academic article titled "The Economics of Salvation: Toward a Theory of Exchange in Chinese Buddhism." This paper was published in 2007 in the Journal of the American Academy of Religion, authored by Professor Michael Walsh of Vassar College.

Let me begin by introducing the background and significance of this research.

In our everyday understanding, religion and economics seem to belong to two entirely different spheres. Religion concerns the spiritual, the faithful, and the transcendentâ€”it is sacred and non-utilitarian. Economics, on the other hand, deals with interests, exchange, and material thingsâ€”it is secular and calculative. We rarely connect these two domains. But Professor Walsh poses a subversive question: How did Buddhism manage to survive in China for two and a half millennia?

This question appears simple but is actually quite profound. Any organizationâ€”whether a business, a political party, or a religious groupâ€”needs to solve the problem of resource acquisition to survive long-term. Buddhist monks do not engage in production, so where do their food, clothing, and shelter come from? The maintenance of monastery buildings, the printing of scriptures, the worship of Buddha statuesâ€”all these require substantial resources. Professor Walsh's answer is that Buddhism solved this problem through an elaborate "exchange mechanism," which he calls "the economics of salvation."

What makes this paper particularly distinctive is its synthesis of theoretical resources from multiple disciplines. French anthropologist Marcel Mauss's theory in "The Gift" tells us that gift-giving is not selfless but creates social obligations. French philosopher Jacques Derrida further argues for the impossibility of a "pure gift." German economist Karl Marx's commodity theory helps us understand how value is generated through exchange. And French sociologist Pierre Bourdieu's concept of capital shows us how economic capital transforms into cultural capital and social prestige. This interdisciplinary theoretical synthesis is one of the paper's major strengths.

So how does Professor Walsh develop his argument? He chooses a very clever entry point: a stele inscription from the Southern Song period.

In the late twelfth century, around the 1190s, the famous poet Lu You wrote a stele inscription for Ayuwang Monastery in Zhejiang Province. Ayuwang Monastery is a renowned Buddhist sacred site, said to house a reliquary stupa from the time of King Asoka. The inscription records the following story: In 1131, Emperor Gaozong, while fleeing southward, passed by Ayuwang Monastery and personally inscribed calligraphy as a gift to the monastery, also granting permission for the monastery to purchase land. Fifty years later, Chan Master Deguang finally realized this vision, using donations from the emperor, high officials, and Buddhist patrons to purchase vast tracts of farmland, with annual harvests reaching 5,000 bushels. Lu You writes that Deguang "established blessings for all the world and enabled them to multiply."

Professor Walsh keenly observes that this short stele inscription actually contains six distinct "transactions." First, emperors donate their calligraphy, and in exchange, the monastery and surrounding area flourish. Second, a formal relationship of protection is established between the imperial house and the monastery. Third, monks perform blessing rituals for the imperial family. Fourth, monks commit to "repaying the emperor's benevolence." Fifth, Master Deguang "establishes merit and causes it to multiply." Sixth, the monastery purchases substantial amounts of land.

These seemingly pious religious acts actually constitute a complete economic exchange system. The emperor invests calligraphy, money, and political support; he receives religious legitimacy and blessing rituals. The monastery invests religious services and the bestowal of merit; it receives land, money, and imperial protection. This is what Walsh calls "the economics of salvation"â€”an economic system for exchanging material resources for spiritual salvation.

The innovation of this research lies in breaking down the dichotomy between religion and economics, redefining "merit"â€”this seemingly purely spiritual conceptâ€”as a kind of "commodity." But please note: this is not meant to denigrate religion or deny the sincerity of religious faith. What Professor Walsh emphasizes is that understanding religion's economic foundation is not about dissolving religion, but about more completely understanding how religion operates as a social phenomenon.

For us today, this research also has contemporary significance. It helps us understand how modern religious organizationsâ€”whether Buddhist temples, Christian churches, or other religious groupsâ€”operate. It also prompts us to reconsider the social function behind so-called "selfless giving": Why do people donate to religious organizations? What return do they expect?

Now let me hand over to the second speaker, who will explain the institutional exchange relationship between imperial power and Buddhism.

This section primarily discusses the fifth transaction, namely how Chan Master Deguang "established merit and enabled it to multiply." In this section, we will explore what merit actually is, and how it was understood, produced, and accumulated.

First, I want to introduce a core concept: "field of merit," or futian in Chinese. This term appears frequently in Chinese Buddhist literature. The Sanskrit original means "field of merit," which translates directly in English as "field of merit." This is an ingeniously crafted agricultural metaphor.

Imagine this: when you donate to the Buddhist sangha, it's like sowing seeds in a fertile field. The seeds will grow, sprout, flower, and bear fruit, ultimately providing you with returns far exceeding your initial investment. As anthropologist Stanley Tambiah puts it: "Any donation to the Buddhist sangha is like planting a seed in the 'great field of merit'â€”it will grow and provide the donor with more than they originally donated."

Why did Buddhists choose agricultural metaphors to explain merit? This is closely related to China's cultural context. China has been an agrarian society since ancient times, and terms like "cultivating," "planting," "sowing," and "harvesting" are familiar to everyone. Using these terms to explain abstract religious concepts made them understandable and acceptable even to ordinary farmers. Professor Walsh points out that Buddhists clearly knew who their target audience was when choosing their communication strategies. This was a very clever localization strategy.

Next, I want to distinguish between two frequently confused concepts: karma and merit. Both are very important in Buddhism, but they have essential differences.

Karma, in Sanskrit, refers to the results of one's own actions. If you do good deeds, you accumulate good karma; if you do evil deeds, you accumulate bad karma. Karma emphasizes personal good intentions and internal motivationâ€”it is a personal, internal accumulation.

But merit is different. Merit can be obtained with the help of third partiesâ€”such as monks. It emphasizes not just good intentions but, more importantly, proper action. And most crucially, merit is transferable.

This point is very important and is one of the most distinctive practices in Chinese Buddhism. One scholar used a very vivid metaphor: merit is like a "spiritual bank account" from which you can make payments to others. For example, you can "dedicate" the merit you've accumulated to deceased relatives, helping them obtain better circumstances in their next life. This practice is very popular in Chinese ancestral worship cultureâ€”many people attend Buddhist ceremonies and donate to temples specifically to accumulate merit for their deceased parents or ancestors.

Even more interesting is a seemingly paradoxical point: transferring merit not only doesn't reduce your own merit, but actually increases it. It's like one candle lighting another candle without diminishing its own flame. This concept is explicitly recorded in Pali Buddhist scriptures and was further developed in Chinese Buddhism.

So how can ordinary people earn merit? The Buddhist Canon records seven methods: help build temples or pagodas, plant trees, give medicine to the ill, build sturdy boats, repair bridges, build irrigation canals, and build public toilets.

Looking carefully at these seven methods, you'll notice they share a common feature: each action provides tangible benefit to others. This shows that merit is not some ethereal abstract concept, but is closely connected to concrete good deeds. Scholar Stephen Teiser points out in his research that merit brings "concrete blessingsâ€”long life and wealth." This is not a promise for the afterlife, but benefits in this life.

Professor Walsh draws on several scholars' concepts to summarize this point. Japanese scholars Reader and Tanabe call this "practical religiosity," meaning that religion is not just metaphysical contemplation but a means of solving practical problems. Dutch scholar Barend ter Haar calls it a "Buddhist-inspired option," meaning Buddhism provides ordinary people with a path to obtaining salvific value.

Whatever we call it, the core meaning is the same: for ordinary donors, accumulating merit was a very pragmatic strategy. They faced various daily problemsâ€”illness, financial difficulties, poor harvestsâ€”as well as ultimate concernsâ€”death and the afterlife. Merit provided a path to solving these problems. This is why Lu You's stele inscription says Master Deguang was able to "establish merit and enable it to multiply"â€”merit can be produced, accumulated, and increased, just like grain.

As the paper states: "The experience of merit and merit-making was a material exchange process, and at the heart of this exchange process was the practice of seeking, attaining, and accumulating this-worldly benefits." This sentence precisely captures the core argument of this section: merit is not a purely spiritual concept, but a material entity that can be exchanged.

Thank you to the third speaker for that excellent explanation of "The Experience of Merit." Now I will introduce the fourth section of the paperâ€”"Salvation in the Fields." This section focuses on the sixth transaction from the stele inscription: the monastery purchasing land. This is the most material element of the entire "economics of salvation."

From the fifth century CE onward, land donations became an important form of "good deeds" in Chinese Buddhism. French sinologist Jacques Gernet, in his classic study, detailed the economic history of Chinese Buddhist monasteries and found that monasteries acquired land in many ways. First, through imperial gifts or grants, as in the Ayuwang Monastery example. Second, through donor donationsâ€”this was the primary source. Third, through purchases, where monasteries used accumulated funds to buy land. Fourth, through mortgages, where people mortgaged their land to monasteries for loans and eventually couldn't redeem it. Fifth, Professor Walsh also acknowledges, monasteries sometimes illegally occupied ownerless land.

Regardless of how it was obtained, land was a monastery's most important asset. A simple but key exchange logic operates here: donors gave land or harvests to monasteries, and in return, monasteries awarded donors merit. Professor Walsh points out that donating land was "the most enduring institutional act a donor could make"â€”because land can be passed down through generations, its benefits are permanent, far more lasting and reliable than a one-time monetary donation.

But here lies what I call "the wonderful irony of Buddhism." One of Buddhism's core teachings is relinquishing attachment and transcending material desire. Monks leave their families, renounce property, and live simple lives. Yet through the merit exchange mechanism, Buddhist monasteries instead accumulated vast material wealth, becoming some of the largest landowners in Chinese history. How can this be explained?

The paper points out that monasteries rarely directly addressed the sensitive topic of "wealth accumulation." Their argument focused instead on this: land donations were for "feeding monks." Monks don't engage in production and need lay support; land produces grain, grain supports monks, monks pray for believersâ€”a perfect circular argument. Scholar Alan Cole uses a very clever formulation to describe this argumentative strategy: Buddhists convinced farmers that crops growing from the ground were actually derived from past moral actions, and that future food supplies could only be secured by exchanging some of this food for merit. In other words, if you want a good harvest next year, you must donate grain to the monastery this year. Meritâ€”this "invisible and rather magical product"â€”was the Buddhists' specialty.

Tang dynasty monk Zongmi provides a brilliant interpretation. He says: "It is like worldly people who want to obtain a granary so abundantly stocked with the five grains that they are never in want. They must gather the seeds from grain, use an ox and plow to till the fields, and plant the seeds...This is called 'planting merit.' If they do not plant merit, they will be poor; lacking merit and wisdom, they will enter the dangerous path of birth-and-death."

This passage completely agriculturalizes the abstract concept of merit. It uses things ordinary farmers do every dayâ€”selecting seeds, plowing fields, sowing seedsâ€”to explain the logic of merit. If you don't sow, you can't cultivate; if you don't cultivate, you have no harvest. Fields can be fertile or barrenâ€”this depends on whether you choose the right "field of merit" to sow in. And what is the right "field of merit"? Of course, Buddhist monasteries. So the conclusion is: donate to monasteries, sow good seeds, perform good deeds, earn merit, protect your family, and ensure a good harvest next year.

By the Song dynasty, literatus Ma Tingluan further developed this thinking, and a very interesting conceptual fusion occurred. He writes: "If your heart is purified so too will Amitabha Buddha's land be pure. This is the teaching of the Pure Land." Here, "field of merit" (futian, the merit field, real farmland) and "Pure Land" (Amitabha Buddha's Western Paradise) are unified. Cultivating the mind is internal work; donating land is external action, but both ultimately lead to the same goalâ€”the Pure Land. The more land you donate, the purer your mind becomes; the purer your mind, the closer you are to the Pure Land. This is a very ingenious religious argument.

Ma Tingluan also makes a very practical point. He says: "Among all donations given to the monastery the most important has been land." Why is land most important? Because land is a permanent asset. He continues: "Who, over a period of a thousand years, can take care of their land without destroying or ruining it? The Buddhist family, the monks, the Buddhist monasteries can do this." What does this mean? Ordinary families divide property within three generations and decline within five; no one can guarantee their land will remain unchanged for a thousand years. But monasteries are differentâ€”monasteries are institutions that transcend individual lifespans and can continue forever. So if you want your merit to last forever, you should donate your land to a monastery, not leave it to your descendants.

This section shows us how Buddhism combined abstract salvific ideas with concrete land economics to form a powerful argumentative system. This system has both religious sacredness and economic practicalityâ€”no wonder it could persist for a millennium.

First, let me introduce French anthropologist Marcel Mauss. Mauss was Durkheim's nephew and academic successor. His 1925 book, "The Gift: Forms and Functions of Exchange in Archaic Societies," remains a classic of social science, still widely cited today.

Mauss studied gift exchange phenomena around the worldâ€”from the "potlatch" of North American indigenous peoples to the "kula ring" of Pacific islandsâ€”and reached a subversive conclusion: gift-giving is not "selfless" but creates social obligations.

Mauss argued that gift exchange involves three compulsory obligations: the obligation to give, the obligation to receive, and the obligation to reciprocate. When I give you a gift, you must accept itâ€”refusing a gift is a social offense, meaning you refuse to establish a relationship with me. And once you accept the gift, you owe me a debtâ€”you must reciprocate at an appropriate time. So Mauss says, "to make a gift of something to someone is to make a present of some part of oneself"â€”the gift carries part of the giver's spirit, and the recipient must reciprocate, or this spirit will cause trouble.

Behind gift exchange lies competition, rivalry, and ostentationâ€”it's a power game. Whoever gives more occupies the moral and social high ground. Next is French philosopher Jacques Derrida. Derrida was a representative figure of deconstruction. In his 1992 book "Given Time," he pushes Mauss's analysis to a more radical logical conclusion.

Derrida poses a sharp question: If a gift requires reciprocation, is it still a true gift? He argues that a true gift must be completely selflessâ€”expecting no return, not even gratitude. But the problem is, once a gift is given, accepted, and recognized as a gift, it automatically activates the obligation to reciprocate. Derrida uses a very elegant formulation: "If it [the gift] presents itself, it no longer presents itself"â€”the gift, upon appearing, annuls itself.
