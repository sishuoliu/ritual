Good morning/afternoon everyone. Today our group will present Michael Walsh's article "The Economics of Salvation: Toward a Theory of Exchange in Chinese Buddhism." But unlike a typical paper presentation, we aim not only to explain this paper itself, but also to situate it within a broader academic lineage, helping you understand how this research field developed.

First, let me introduce the question: "Who studies religious economics?"

The relationship between religion and economics was long considered belonging to two separate spheres. But academic exploration of this question goes back over a century. In 1867, Marx published Capital, proposing commodity theory and distinguishing use-value from exchange-valueâ€”these became important tools for analyzing religious exchange. In 1925, French anthropologist Marcel Mauss published The Gift, arguing that so-called "selfless giving" actually creates social obligationsâ€”directly challenging naive understandings of religious donations.

For Buddhist economic history specifically, the most important founder is French sinologist Jacques Gernet. His Buddhism in Chinese Society: An Economic History from the Fifth to the Tenth Centuries, published in French in 1956 and English in 1995, is the foundational work in this field. Through extensive historical sources, Gernet demonstrated that Chinese Buddhist monasteries were not otherworldly retreats, but major economic forces in medieval China. Monasteries owned vast lands, engaged in commercial operations, and even conducted financial activitiesâ€”lending money and running pawnshops. This discovery fundamentally transformed our understanding of Chinese Buddhism.

Walsh's research stands on these giants' shoulders. His unique contribution lies in synthesizing four major theoretical resources: Marx's commodity theory, Mauss's gift theory, Bourdieu's capital theory, and Derrida's gift philosophy.

Let me briefly explain how these four theories integrate into Walsh's analysis. Marx tells us commodities have use-value and exchange-valueâ€”Walsh argues merit has both. Mauss tells us gifts create reciprocal obligationsâ€”Walsh argues merit exchange is precisely such a reciprocal relationship. Bourdieu tells us economic capital converts to cultural capitalâ€”Walsh uses the "stele inscription" example to demonstrate this conversion. Derrida tells us pure gifts are logically impossibleâ€”Walsh argues merit is never given unconditionally.

Walsh's paper uses a specific case as its entry point: a stele inscription written by the famous Southern Song poet Lu You for Ayuwang Monastery in the late twelfth century. This inscription records six transactions: emperors bestowing calligraphy, imperial-monastic relations being established, monks praying for the emperor, monks repaying imperial benevolence, Master Deguang establishing merit, and the monastery purchasing land. Walsh keenly observes that these seemingly religious acts actually constitute a complete economic exchange system.

The paper has five sections: "Merit and the Institution" discusses the reciprocal relationship between imperial house and monastery; "The Experience of Merit" analyzes how merit was understood and accumulated; "Salvation in the Fields" explores the logic of land donations; "The Problem with Giving" uses Mauss and Derrida to analyze the gift-exchange distinction; "Commodity, Capital, and Exchange" uses Marx and Bourdieu to summarize merit's economic properties.

My colleagues will explore from the following angles: Speaker 2 will combine Tambiah's "field of merit" research to explain merit's nature; Speaker 3 will combine Gernet and Schopen's research to explain land and institution; Speaker 4 will delve into Mauss and Derrida's theories on the gift problem; Speaker 5 will synthesize everything and look toward future directions in religious economics.

Thank you to the first speaker for outlining the academic context. Now I'll explain the nature of merit, focusing particularly on the scholarly development from Stanley Tambiah to Michael Walsh.

Before understanding Walsh's paper, we must understand the anthropological tradition he inherited and developedâ€”especially Tambiah's research.

Stanley Tambiah was a Sri Lankan-American anthropologist and Harvard professor. His 1970 book Buddhism and the Spirit Cults in North-east Thailand is a classic study of South Asian Buddhist merit practice. Tambiah proposed a core concept: the sangha as "field of merit."

This concept is ingenious. Tambiah writes: "The monk has an obligation to be a 'field of merit'â€”donors plant seeds in this field, the seeds grow, providing donors with returns exceeding their original investment." This is an agricultural society metaphor: you donate to the monastery like sowing in fertile soil, and you receive harvest returns.

But Tambiah's important insight goes further. He points out that monastic-lay relations are not one-way charity but bidirectional reciprocity. Young men become monks and serve elders facing death; elders support monasteries to accumulate merit. This is actually intergenerational exchangeâ€”young people trade religious service for social recognition, elders trade material support for afterlife blessings.

Another important academic issue is "merit transfer." According to orthodox Buddhist doctrine, each person is responsible for their own karma, emphasizing self-liberation. But in actual folk practice, merit can be transferred to othersâ€”especially to deceased relatives, called "dedication." This seems doctrinally contradictory.

British scholar Richard Gombrich published an important 1971 paper studying merit transfer in Sinhalese Buddhism. He discovered an interesting paradox: when you transfer merit to others, your own merit doesn't decrease but actually increases. Like one candle lighting another without diminishing its own flame.

Now let's see how Walsh advances beyond these predecessors. Tambiah analyzed merit from an anthropological perspective, emphasizing it as "social exchange"; Walsh analyzes merit from a religious economics perspective, arguing it is "commodity exchange." This is an important conceptual shift.

Walsh's key innovation is applying Marx's commodity theory to merit. He argues merit is not just a "seed in the field" but a commodity. Commodities have use-value (merit brings blessings), exchange-value (merit can be traded for land, rituals, status), and are products of labor (monks' practice and prayer).

As the paper states: "The experience of merit and merit-making was a material exchange process, and at the heart of this exchange process was the practice of seeking, attaining, and accumulating this-worldly benefits."

This is the scholarly development from Tambiah to Walsh: from "field metaphor" to "commodity analysis," from anthropological description to economic explanation.
